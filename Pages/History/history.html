The history of the Fire Department Connection (FDC) in New York City is deeply tied to the broader evolution of fire safety measures in the city. As fire protection systems advanced over time, the FDC became an essential component of ensuring that fire departments could access and supplement a building's internal fire suppression systems during emergencies.

1. Development of Fire Safety in New York: In the 19th century, as New York City grew in size and density, devastating fires became a significant concern. Early firefighting efforts relied on rudimentary equipment like horse-drawn water wagons and bucket brigades. By the late 1800s, buildings were increasingly being outfitted with standpipes and sprinklers, but there was a need for external connections to allow firefighters to pump water directly into these systems.

2. Introduction of the Standpipe System: Standpipe systems were introduced in New York City in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, primarily in larger and taller buildings, which were becoming more common with the rise of skyscrapers. These systems allowed firefighters to connect hoses to standpipe outlets on various floors rather than relying solely on their own hoses from the ground. However, there was a need for a connection at street level for fire engines to pump water into the system—this led to the development of the FDC.

3. The Role of Sprinklers and FDC: As automatic sprinklers became more widespread in the early 20th century, especially after the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire in 1911, the FDC became crucial. Fire Department Connections allowed firefighters to boost water pressure to sprinkler systems when a fire was too intense for the automatic system alone.

4. Standardization and Modernization: Over time, regulations and standards regarding FDCs were established, driven by the growing complexity of New York's building codes. The city’s fire department worked closely with architects and engineers to ensure that FDCs were accessible, standardized, and clearly marked. This ensured that during emergencies, firefighters could quickly identify and use them to supply additional water pressure to a building's internal firefighting system.

5. Contemporary Use: Today, FDCs are a mandated part of most mid- to high-rise buildings in New York. The New York City Fire Code, maintained by the FDNY, provides specific guidelines on the installation, maintenance, and signage of FDCs. These connections allow fire engines to deliver water to sprinkler or standpipe systems, reinforcing the building's built-in fire protection mechanisms.

In essence, the FDC is a crucial part of New York's layered fire safety strategy, ensuring that firefighters can quickly and effectively respond to fires in modern, often complex urban buildings.
